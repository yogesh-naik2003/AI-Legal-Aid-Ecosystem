Your Six Fundamental Consumer Rights (COPRA 2019)

The **Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (COPRA 2019)** significantly enhanced consumer rights, introducing provisions for **e-commerce** and redefining key terms like **product liability**. Understanding these rights is the basis for any complaint.

The Six Consumer Rights

  • Right to Safety: Protection against the marketing of goods and services which are hazardous to life and property.
  • Right to be Informed: Right to be informed about the quality, quantity, potency, standard, and price of goods or services.
  • Right to Choose: Access to a variety of goods and services at competitive prices.
  • Right to be Heard: Right to ensure that consumer interests receive due consideration at appropriate forums.
  • Right to Seek Redressal: Right to seek compensation against **unfair trade practices** or unscrupulous exploitation.
  • Right to Consumer Education: Right to acquire the knowledge and skill to be an informed consumer.

New Concepts under COPRA 2019

The new Act explicitly includes **e-commerce transactions** and introduced the concept of **Product Liability**, holding manufacturers or sellers responsible for harm caused by a defective product.

Navigating the Three-Tier Consumer Redressal Mechanism

Consumer complaints are resolved through a quasi-judicial system, avoiding the delays of civil courts. The appropriate forum is determined solely by the pecuniary value (monetary value) of the goods/services and compensation claimed.

Jurisdiction Based on Pecuniary Value

  1. District Commission: Handles complaints where the value of the claim **does not exceed ₹1 Crore**.
  2. State Commission: Handles complaints where the claim value **exceeds ₹1 Crore but does not exceed ₹10 Crore**. Also hears appeals against District Commission orders.
  3. National Commission: Handles complaints where the claim value **exceeds ₹10 Crore**. Also hears appeals against State Commission orders.

Key Procedural Rules

  • Limitation Period: A complaint must be filed within **two years** from the date the cause of action arose.
  • E-Filing: Complaints can now be filed electronically, making the process more accessible.
  • Jurisdiction (Territorial): A complaint can be filed where the defendant resides OR where the cause of action arose OR where the complainant resides.

Step-by-Step: Filing a Consumer Complaint

A consumer complaint must be carefully prepared, detailing the deficiency and supported by concrete evidence. The first step, however, is almost always approaching the seller directly.

The Six-Step Filing Process

  1. Send Legal Notice: Before filing in the forum, send a formal **legal notice** (or a detailed letter) to the seller/manufacturer demanding rectification or compensation.
  2. Draft the Complaint: Clearly state the complainant's identity, the facts, the grounds of complaint (e.g., **Deficiency in Service** or **Defect in Goods**), and the **Relief Sought** (e.g., refund, replacement, or compensation).
  3. Affix Documents: Attach copies of all supporting documents (**bills, warranties, correspondence, legal notice copy**).
  4. Pay Fee: A nominal fee must be paid via Demand Draft or online, varying by the value of the claim.
  5. Filing: Submit the complaint (either physically or through the e-filing portal) to the appropriate Commission based on pecuniary jurisdiction.
  6. Hearing: Once accepted, the Commission issues notice to the opposite party, initiating the process of submitting written replies and evidence.

Available Reliefs (Orders the Commission Can Give)

  • Directing the opposite party to remove the defect or deficiency.
  • Ordering a refund of the price paid.
  • Awarding compensation for any loss or injury suffered.
  • Discontinuing **Unfair Trade Practices** permanently.

Tackling Unfair Trade Practices and Misleading Ads

COPRA 2019 places significant focus on regulating practices that deceive consumers, especially through online and digital advertisements. The Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) plays a key role in enforcing these provisions.

Defining Unfair Trade Practices (UTP)

  • Misleading Advertisements: Any ad that falsely describes a product, guarantees results without adequate testing, or deliberately conceals important information.
  • Bait Advertising: Advertising goods/services at very low prices to attract customers, only to switch them to more expensive products later.
  • Spurious Goods: Products falsely labeled as genuine products of another manufacturer.

The Role of the CCPA

The CCPA has the power to:

  1. Issue guidelines for preventing unfair trade practices.
  2. Order an immediate withdrawal or modification of misleading advertisements.
  3. Impose penalties on manufacturers, endorsers, and publishers (including fines up to ₹50 Lakh) for false advertising.

Consumer Rights in the Digital Age (E-Commerce)

The 2019 Act brought clarity and accountability to online shopping. E-commerce entities and marketplaces are now held responsible for disclosing complete product information and ensuring return/refund policies are clearly defined.

Specific E-Commerce Requirements

  • Mandatory Disclosures: Platforms must disclose the seller's details, contact information, customer care numbers, and grievance redressal mechanism.
  • No Arbitrary Cancellation: Sellers cannot arbitrarily cancel confirmed orders unless exceptional circumstances exist.
  • Country of Origin: E-commerce entities must clearly display the 'country of origin' for all products.

Liability for Marketplace Models

Even in a marketplace model (where the platform connects buyers and sellers), the e-commerce entity can be held liable if they fail to disclose sufficient information about the seller, leading to the consumer's inability to seek redressal.

The Procedure for Registering a First Information Report (FIR)

An **FIR** is a written document prepared by police when they receive information about the commission of a cognizable offense. It is the crucial starting point for any criminal investigation and is governed by **Section 154 of the CrPC (Code of Criminal Procedure)**.

Key Concepts & Terms

  • Cognizable Offense: A serious offense where the police officer **can arrest without a warrant**. Police *must* register an FIR if reported.
  • Non-Cognizable Offense (NCR): A less serious offense where police **cannot arrest without a warrant**. Police simply record the information in a General Diary (**NCR** entry) and advise the complainant to approach a Magistrate.
  • Zero FIR: An FIR registered at *any* police station, regardless of jurisdiction. It is later transferred to the correct police station.

What to Do If Police Refuse to Register an FIR

If the offense is cognizable and police refuse registration, follow these legal avenues:

  1. Approach Superior Officer (Sec. 154(3) CrPC): Send the written complaint to the **Superintendent of Police (SP)** by speed post/registered mail.
  2. Approach the Magistrate (Sec. 156(3) CrPC): File a private complaint before a Judicial Magistrate, who can direct the police to register the FIR and investigate the matter.
  3. Judicial Remedy: Courts have the power to penalize police officers for unjustified refusal.

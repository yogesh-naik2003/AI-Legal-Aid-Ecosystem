Cheque Bounce:
The Legal Recourse

A step-by-step guide on the legal procedure for a dishonoured cheque case under **Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881**.

Essential Ingredients for a Case

For a cheque bounce to be a criminal offense under Section 138, the following conditions must be met:

  1. The cheque must be drawn for the discharge of a legally enforceable debt or liability.
  2. The cheque must be presented to the bank within **three months** from the date it was issued.
  3. The cheque is returned unpaid by the bank due to **insufficient funds** or other reasons like 'payment stopped'.
  4. The payee (the person to whom the cheque is issued) makes a demand for payment by giving a **legal notice** in writing to the drawer (the person who issued the cheque) within **30 days** of receiving the 'cheque return memo' from the bank.
  5. The drawer fails to make the payment within **15 days** of the receipt of the said notice.

Step-by-Step Legal Procedure

If the drawer fails to pay after the notice, you can initiate legal proceedings. The process is time-bound and requires precision.

  • Step 1: Send a Legal Notice: A formal demand notice must be sent via a lawyer to the drawer's correct address within 30 days of receiving the 'cheque return memo'. It must demand payment of the cheque amount within 15 days.
  • Step 2: File a Complaint: If the drawer does not pay within 15 days of receiving the notice, you can file a criminal complaint under Section 138 before the appropriate Magistrate's court. This must be done within one month from the expiry of the 15-day notice period.
  • Step 3: Court Process: The court will examine the complaint and documents. If a prima facie case is made out, it will issue a summons to the accused. The trial will then proceed, involving evidence and arguments from both sides.

Crucial Timelines to Remember

Adherence to timelines is mandatory. Failure to comply can make your case invalid.

  • 3 Months: The cheque must be presented to the bank within 3 months of its issue date.
  • 30 Days: The legal notice must be sent to the drawer within 30 days of receiving the 'cheque return memo' from the bank.
  • 15 Days: The drawer has 15 days from the receipt of the notice to make the payment.
  • 1 Month: A complaint must be filed in court within one month after the 15-day notice period expires.

Criminal Complaint vs. Civil Suit (Dual Remedy)

The dishonour of a cheque offers the creditor two distinct avenues of action, which can be pursued simultaneously to maximize recovery and apply pressure on the debtor.

The Two Legal Paths

1. Criminal Complaint (Sec. 138 NI Act)
  • Purpose: To punish the drawer (imprisonment/fine).
  • Remedy: Fine (up to double the cheque amount) and compensation.
  • Jurisdiction: Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC).
  • Timeline: Very strict (as per 30, 15, 1 month rule).
2. Civil Suit for Recovery
  • Purpose: To recover the specific debt amount with interest.
  • Remedy: Repayment of principal debt + interest.
  • Jurisdiction: Civil Court or Commercial Court.
  • Timeline: Statute of Limitation is typically **3 years** from the date the cause of action arose.

Note: Courts allow both actions to run concurrently, but the amount recovered in the criminal case will be adjusted against the final decree in the civil suit.

Punishment & Interim Compensation

The law provides for stringent penalties to deter the issuance of bad cheques and also provides for interim relief to the complainant.

Penalties under Section 138

  • Imprisonment: For a term which may extend to **two years**.
  • Fine: An amount which may extend to **twice the amount of the cheque**.
  • Both: The court has the discretion to impose both imprisonment and a fine.

Interim Compensation (Section 143A)

A significant amendment allows the court to order the drawer to pay **interim compensation** to the complainant during the trial. This amount can be up to 20% of the cheque amount and must be paid within 60 days of the order. If the drawer is acquitted, the complainant must refund this amount with interest.

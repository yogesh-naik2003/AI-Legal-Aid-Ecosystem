"Digital Arrest" Scams:
How to Protect Yourself

A guide to recognizing this sophisticated cybercrime where scammers impersonate law enforcement to extort money through fear and intimidation.

What is a "Digital Arrest" Scam?

A "Digital Arrest" is a form of cyber extortion where scammers impersonate officials from legitimate bodies like the Police, CBI, ED, or TRAI. They create a fake sense of urgency and authority, convincing the victim that they are implicated in a serious crime (e.g., money laundering, drug trafficking, or receiving illegal packages).

The scammers then place the victim under "digital arrest," forcing them to stay on a video call (like Skype or WhatsApp) and isolating them from family and friends. The ultimate goal is to coerce the victim into transferring large sums of money under the guise of "verification," "settlement," or "clearing their name."

How the Scam Unfolds: A Step-by-Step Breakdown

  1. The Initial Contact: You receive an automated call or a direct call from someone claiming to be from TRAI, your telecom provider, or a courier service. They allege your mobile number or Aadhaar card has been used for illegal activities.
  2. The Transfer: The call is then "transferred" to a senior "police officer" or "CBI official." This person speaks with authority, uses legal jargon, and may even show a fake ID card over a video call.
  3. The Accusation: They present a fabricated case, often showing a fake FIR or arrest warrant with your name and photo (which they get from your social media profiles).
  4. The "Digital Arrest": You are ordered to remain on a video call, isolated from others, and are forbidden from disconnecting. This creates immense psychological pressure.
  5. The Extortion: The scammer offers a way out – a "verification" or "settlement" process that requires you to transfer a large sum of money to a specified bank account, promising it will be returned after the "investigation."

Red Flags: How to Identify the Scam

  • Impersonation of Authority: Real police or government agencies will NEVER contact you over WhatsApp or conduct an investigation via video call. They will issue an official, physical summons.
  • Demand for Money: No legitimate law enforcement agency will ever ask you to transfer money for "verification," "bail," or to "clear your name." All official payments are made through designated court or government channels.
  • Sense of Urgency & Secrecy: Scammers create panic and forbid you from talking to anyone else. This is a tactic to prevent you from thinking clearly or verifying their claims.
  • Use of Skype/WhatsApp: Official investigations are not conducted over consumer messaging apps.

What to Do If You Are Targeted

  1. Stay Calm and Do Not Panic: The entire scam relies on creating fear. Recognize that this is a pressure tactic.
  2. Disconnect the Call Immediately: Do not engage further. Block the number.
  3. NEVER Transfer Money: No matter how convincing they sound, do not transfer any funds.
  4. Talk to Someone: Immediately inform a family member, friend, or colleague about the call. Discussing it will help you realize it's a scam.
  5. Verify Claims Independently: If they claim to be from a specific police station, find the official number of that station online and call them directly to verify. Do not use any number provided by the scammer.

Legal Recourse & Reporting

If you have been targeted or have lost money, it is crucial to report the incident immediately. This helps law enforcement track the criminals and can aid in recovering funds.

How and Where to Report

  • National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal: The primary method. File a complaint online at www.cybercrime.gov.in.
  • Cyber Crime Helpline: Call the toll-free number **1930** immediately, especially if you have lost money. This initiates the process to freeze the fraudulent transaction.
  • Local Police Station: You can also visit your nearest police station to file an FIR regarding the incident.

Applicable Laws

Such scams involve offenses under the Indian Penal Code (IPC) and the Information Technology (IT) Act, including:

  1. Section 420, IPC (Cheating): For dishonestly inducing the delivery of property (money).
  2. Section 384, IPC (Extortion): For putting a person in fear of injury to dishonestly induce them to deliver property.
  3. Section 66D, IT Act (Cheating by Personation): For cheating by using a computer resource or communication device.

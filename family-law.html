Legal Frameworks for Marriage Registration

Marriage in India is governed primarily by personal religious laws, but two secular acts facilitate formal registration, which is now mandatory in most states. Understanding the correct Act is the first step toward securing marital rights.

Key Acts and Requirements

  • Hindu Marriage Act (HMA), 1955: Applies to Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs. Solemnizes marriage through traditional rituals (**Saptapadi**) and provides for subsequent registration.
  • Special Marriage Act (SMA), 1954: A secular law applicable to all Indian citizens, regardless of religion. It allows for **inter-religious** marriages and solemnizes the union through a civil process after a **30-day public notice** period.
  • Conditions for Valid Marriage: Both parties must be capable of giving valid consent, meet the minimum age (18 for the bride, 21 for the groom), and not fall within the prohibited degrees of relationship (**Sapinda relationship**).

The Registration Process

  1. Filing Application: Apply to the local Marriage Registrar.
  2. Notice Period (SMA only): Mandatory 30-day notice inviting objections. This period is often a point of contention.
  3. Solemnization/Registration: Marriage is registered in the presence of witnesses. The Marriage Certificate is the conclusive proof of marriage.

Understanding the Grounds and Types of Divorce

Divorce can be obtained either by mutual consent (the faster route) or by filing a contested petition based on fault grounds. The specific grounds are defined under the respective marriage acts (HMA or SMA).

Types of Divorce

  • Divorce by Mutual Consent: (Sec. 13B HMA / Sec. 28 SMA). Requires a minimum separation period of **one year** before the petition, followed by a court-mandated **cooling-off period** of 6 to 18 months.
  • Contested Divorce: (Sec. 13 HMA). Filed by one spouse against the other on specific fault grounds, which must be legally proven in court.

Primary Grounds for Contested Divorce

  • Cruelty: Includes both physical and mental cruelty that makes living together impossible. This is the most common and often broad ground.
  • Desertion: The spouse voluntarily abandons the petitioner for a continuous period of at least **two years** without reasonable cause.
  • Adultery: Voluntary sexual intercourse by the respondent with a person other than their spouse.
  • Conversion: The conversion of one spouse to another religion.

Alimony (Permanent) and Maintenance (Interim)

Financial provision for the dependent spouse is a core aspect of divorce and separation. The court aims to ensure the dependent spouse can maintain a lifestyle reasonably comparable to the one enjoyed during the marriage.

Key Concepts & Legal Provisions

  • Interim Maintenance (Pendente Lite): Support ordered by the court *during* proceedings (Sec. 24 HMA / Sec. 36 SMA).
  • Permanent Alimony: A final order at the time of the decree. Can be a **fixed monthly payment** or a **one-time lump-sum payment**.
  • Determining Factors: Courts consider financial status, marriage duration, lifestyle, and party conduct.
  • Wife's Right: A wife may also claim maintenance under **Section 125 of the CrPC** simultaneously for speedy relief.

The Maintenance Process

  1. Filing Petition: Dependent spouse files with income and expense affidavits.
  2. Court Assessment: Court examines affidavits and issues order based on judicial guidelines.
  3. Enforcement: Non-payment can lead to attachment of property or imprisonment.

Child Custody, Guardianship, and Visitation Rights

In all matters concerning children, the paramount consideration for the court is the Welfare of the Child, overriding the wishes of either parent. Governed by the **Guardians and Wards Act, 1890** and the **Hindu Minority and Guardianship Act, 1956**.

Custody, Guardianship, and Visitation

  • Guardianship: Pertains to the child's property and long-term decision-making (education, health).
  • Custody: Refers to the physical care and control of the child. Can be **Sole Custody** or **Joint Custody**.
  • Visitation (Access): The right of the non-custodial parent to spend time with the child via a court-granted schedule.

The welfare principle guides the court's approach:

  1. Interaction: The court may interact with older children to gauge their preference.
  2. Parental Fitness: Courts examine the financial stability, character, and moral integrity of each parent.
  3. Mediation: Custody and visitation matters are highly encouraged to be settled through mediation to reduce trauma on the child.

Protection Against Domestic Violence (PWDVA, 2005)

The **Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (PWDVA), 2005** is a comprehensive civil law that provides immediate and effective relief to women who are victims of domestic abuse, extending beyond physical violence.

Key Provisions and Reliefs

  • Domestic Relationship: Covers persons who have lived together in a **shared household** (includes sisters, mothers, daughters-in-law).
  • Economic Abuse: Includes deprivation of financial resources and disposal of joint assets.
  • Protection Order: Prevents the respondent from committing further acts of violence.
  • Residence Order: Grants the aggrieved person the right to reside in the **shared household**.

Procedure for Filing

  1. Complaint: Files an application before a **Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC)** or Metropolitan Magistrate.
  2. Role of Protection Officer: Assists the woman and prepares a Domestic Incident Report (DIR).
  3. Ex Parte Orders: Court can grant immediate temporary relief if urgent intervention is required.

Injunctions and Restraining Orders

Injunctions are court orders directing a person to either perform a specific act or, more commonly in family law, to refrain from performing an act. These are vital for immediate protection of person or property.

Types and Grounds for Injunctions

  • Temporary Injunction: Granted for a specified period to preserve the status quo (Order 39 CPC).
  • Mandatory Injunction: Orders a person to perform a specific act (e.g., hand over a passport).
  • Grounds for Grant: Court considers: (1) **Prima Facie Case**, (2) **Irreparable Injury**, and (3) **Balance of Convenience**.

Common Uses in Family Law

  1. Restraining Property Sale: Preventing a spouse from selling or mortgaging marital assets during divorce.
  2. Preventing Child Removal: Ordering a spouse not to take a child out of the jurisdiction without court permission.
  3. Preventing Harassment: Issuing a restraining order to stop the respondent from entering the petitioner's residence or workplace.

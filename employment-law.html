The Code on Wages: Salary, Minimum Wage, and Deductions

The Code on Wages, 2019, consolidates laws relating to wages, bonus payments, and equal remuneration. It sets national standards for minimum pay and regulates lawful deductions from an employee's salary.

Key Provisions of the Code on Wages, 2019

  • Minimum Wage: The Code mandates the central government to set a **National Floor Wage**. State governments must ensure their minimum wages are not less than this national floor.
  • Definition of Wages: Clarifies that wages include basic pay, dearness allowance, and retaining allowance, but excludes HRA, bonus, and statutory contributions (like EPF).
  • Equal Remuneration: Prohibits gender discrimination in matters relating to wages for the **same work or work of a similar nature**.
  • Payment Period: Wages must be paid within seven days of the end of the wage period. Full and final settlement upon termination must be done within two working days.

Lawful Deductions

Deductions from wages are strictly regulated and generally cannot exceed 50% of the total wages. Allowable deductions include: Income Tax, EPF/ESI contributions, fines, and recovery of advances.

Employee Social Security: EPF, ESI, and Gratuity

These laws ensure employees have a financial safety net for retirement, illness, and long-term service. Compliance is mandatory for employers who meet specific employee thresholds.

Employees' Provident Fund (EPF) & Pension

  • EPF Act, 1952: Mandatory for establishments with **20 or more employees**. Both the employer and employee contribute 12% of the basic salary (plus DA) to the fund.
  • EPF Calculation: The 12% employer contribution is split between the **EPF Scheme** (3.67%) and the **Employee Pension Scheme (EPS)** (8.33%).
  • UAN: A **Universal Account Number (UAN)** is mandatory for all members to enable portability and easy tracking.

Gratuity and ESI

  1. Payment of Gratuity Act, 1972: An employer must pay gratuity to an employee who has completed **five years of continuous service** upon termination, retirement, or resignation. The amount is calculated at 15 days' wages for every completed year of service.
  2. ESI Act, 1948: Provides social security benefits (medical, sickness, maternity) to employees earning up to a specific wage limit in covered areas.

Legal Procedures for Termination and Retrenchment

The process of firing an employee, especially a workman (non-managerial/supervisory staff), is heavily regulated by the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 to prevent arbitrary removal.

Termination vs. Retrenchment

  • Termination (General): Ending employment based on individual performance or contractual expiry. Requires serving the notice period or paying salary in lieu of notice.
  • Retrenchment (Layoff): Termination for reasons other than punishment (e.g., redundancy). Requires mandatory notice and **retrenchment compensation** (15 days' average pay for every completed year of service).
  • Wrongful Termination: Termination without following due process, making the employer liable for damages or reinstatement.

Dispute Resolution (Industrial Disputes Act, 1947)

  1. Conciliation: A government conciliation officer attempts to mediate the dispute between the employer and employee/union (first step).
  2. Adjudication: If conciliation fails, the dispute is referred to the **Labour Court** or **Industrial Tribunal** for a final, binding decision.

Protection Against Sexual Harassment (PoSH Act, 2013)

The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 is mandatory civil legislation aimed at creating safe working environments for women.

Compliance and Procedure

  • Mandatory Internal Complaints Committee (ICC): Every employer with **10 or more employees** must constitute an ICC. The committee must have an external member and a woman presiding officer.
  • Definition of Sexual Harassment: Includes unwelcome acts such as physical contact, a demand for sexual favours, showing pornography, or making sexually coloured remarks.
  • Scope of Workplace: Covers the office premises, transport provided by the employer, and any place visited by the employee arising out of or during the course of employment (e.g., client meetings).

Redressal and Penalties

  1. Complaint: An aggrieved woman can file a complaint with the ICC within three months of the incident.
  2. Inquiry: The ICC must complete the inquiry within **90 days** and submit its report.
  3. Penalties: Failure to constitute the ICC or non-compliance can result in a fine up to **₹50,000** and cancellation of the business license.

Regulation of Working Hours, Overtime, and Leave Entitlements

Statutory regulation of working conditions ensures humane hours and mandatory rest. This is generally governed by the **Factories Act, 1948** (for factories) and the Shops and Establishments Act (state-specific).

Working Hours and Overtime

  • Maximum Working Hours: Typically **8 hours per day** and **48 hours per week**.
  • Overtime Pay: An employee working beyond the stipulated hours is entitled to compensation at **twice the ordinary rate of wages**.
  • Rest Intervals: Mandatory rest intervals must be provided during the working day.

Leave Entitlements

  1. Annual Leave/Earned Leave (EL): Calculated based on the number of days worked. Generally one day of EL for every 20 days worked.
  2. Sick Leave/Casual Leave: Varies by state law, but usually mandatory for all workers.
  3. Maternity Leave: The **Maternity Benefit Act, 1961** mandates up to **26 weeks of paid leave** for women employees, provided certain conditions are met.

The Legal Weight of the Appointment Letter

The Appointment Letter is the primary contract establishing the employer-employee relationship. It formalizes all terms of service and is crucial for resolving disputes related to salary, designation, and termination.

Critical Clauses in the Appointment Contract

  • Probation Period: Defines the initial testing period (usually 3 to 6 months) during which termination can be easier for the employer.
  • Notice Period: Specifies the mandatory period (e.g., 30, 60, or 90 days) required by either party to terminate the contract. Can be substituted by **Pay in Lieu of Notice**.
  • Non-Compete and Non-Solicitation: Clauses designed to protect the employer's business interests. Note that post-employment non-compete clauses are generally unenforceable in India.
  • Confidentiality Clause: Binds the employee to protect the company's proprietary information, both during and after employment.

Legal Basis

Terms and conditions outlined in the Appointment Letter are enforceable under the Indian Contract Act, 1872, provided they do not violate any mandatory provisions of labour law (e.g., Minimum Wage). Labour laws always override any contractual term that is less favourable to the employee.
